# Legal Jargon Glossary for Jeff Mills Case

## Essential Legal Terms You Need to Know

### Court Basics

**Plaintiff**
- **Definition**: The person who files the lawsuit (you, Jeff Mills)
- **Example**: "Jeff Mills is the plaintiff in this case"

**Defendant**
- **Definition**: The person or entity being sued
- **Example**: "The landlord is the defendant"

**Jurisdiction**
- **Definition**: The authority of a court to hear a case; also refers to the geographic area
- **Example**: "This case falls under conciliation court jurisdiction because the amount is under $15,000"

**Venue**
- **Definition**: The specific court location where a case should be filed
- **Example**: "The proper venue is the county where the defendant resides"

**Standing**
- **Definition**: Your legal right to bring a lawsuit
- **Example**: "As the tenant, you have standing to sue for housing violations"

### Case Process Terms

**Affidavit**
- **Definition**: A written statement made under oath that facts are true
- **Example**: "The Affidavit of Inability to Pay allows you to request a fee waiver"
- **Important**: Making false statements in an affidavit is perjury (a crime)

**Statement of Claim**
- **Definition**: A document that explains what happened, who is responsible, and what you want
- **Example**: "Your Statement of Claim should clearly state the damages you suffered"

**Service of Process**
- **Definition**: Officially delivering legal documents to the other party
- **Example**: "The defendant must be served with the complaint within 60 days"

**Summons**
- **Definition**: A document that notifies the defendant they're being sued and must respond
- **Example**: "The summons tells the defendant when to appear in court"

**Filing Fee**
- **Definition**: Money paid to the court to file a case
- **Example**: "Conciliation court filing fees range from $50-$100 depending on claim amount"

**Fee Waiver**
- **Definition**: Permission to file without paying fees due to financial hardship
- **Example**: "Complete the Affidavit of Inability to Pay to request a fee waiver"

### Evidence Terms

**Admissible Evidence**
- **Definition**: Evidence that can legally be presented in court
- **Example**: "Photos with timestamps are usually admissible evidence"

**Hearsay**
- **Definition**: A statement made outside of court, offered to prove the truth of what it asserts
- **Example**: "You can't testify about what someone else told you they saw - that's hearsay"
- **Note**: There are many exceptions to the hearsay rule

**Burden of Proof**
- **Definition**: The obligation to prove your claims
- **Example**: "As the plaintiff, you have the burden of proof to show the defendant caused damages"

**Preponderance of Evidence**
- **Definition**: The standard of proof in civil cases - more likely than not (51% or greater)
- **Example**: "You must show it's more likely than not that the defendant breached the lease"

**Discovery**
- **Definition**: The pre-trial process of exchanging evidence and information
- **Example**: "During discovery, you can request documents from the defendant"

**Deposition**
- **Definition**: Sworn testimony taken before trial, usually in a lawyer's office
- **Example**: "The defendant's lawyer may want to take your deposition"

### Common Legal Actions

**Motion**
- **Definition**: A formal request to the court for a ruling or order
- **Example**: "A motion to dismiss asks the court to throw out the case"

**Continuance**
- **Definition**: A postponement of a court date
- **Example**: "You can request a continuance if you need more time to prepare"

**Default Judgment**
- **Definition**: A ruling in your favor because the defendant didn't respond or appear
- **Example**: "If the defendant doesn't answer the summons, you can request a default judgment"

**Settlement**
- **Definition**: An agreement to resolve the case without going to trial
- **Example**: "Many cases settle before trial to avoid court costs"

**Mediation**
- **Definition**: A process where a neutral third party helps both sides reach an agreement
- **Example**: "The court may require mediation before allowing a trial"

**Arbitration**
- **Definition**: A process where a neutral person hears both sides and makes a binding decision
- **Example**: "Some rental agreements require arbitration instead of court"

### Housing/Tenant Law Specific Terms

**Lease/Rental Agreement**
- **Definition**: A contract between landlord and tenant
- **Example**: "The lease specifies what repairs the landlord must make"

**Habitability**
- **Definition**: The legal requirement that rental housing be safe and livable
- **Example**: "Lack of heat in winter violates the implied warranty of habitability"

**Constructive Eviction**
- **Definition**: When conditions are so bad that the tenant is forced to move out
- **Example**: "Continuous flooding could constitute constructive eviction"

**Security Deposit**
- **Definition**: Money paid upfront to cover potential damages
- **Example**: "The landlord must return the security deposit within 21 days"

**Retaliation**
- **Definition**: Negative action by a landlord in response to a tenant asserting their rights
- **Example**: "Evicting you after you complained about violations is illegal retaliation"

**Quiet Enjoyment**
- **Definition**: The tenant's right to use and enjoy the property without interference
- **Example**: "Constant harassment by the landlord violates your right to quiet enjoyment"

### Damages and Remedies

**Compensatory Damages**
- **Definition**: Money to compensate for actual losses
- **Example**: "You can claim compensatory damages for moving costs and rent overpayment"

**Punitive Damages**
- **Definition**: Money awarded to punish the defendant (rarely available in civil cases)
- **Example**: "Punitive damages may be available if the landlord's conduct was malicious"

**Actual Damages**
- **Definition**: Proven financial losses with documentation
- **Example**: "Receipts for hotel stays due to uninhabitable conditions are actual damages"

**Treble Damages**
- **Definition**: Triple the actual damages (available in some housing cases)
- **Example**: "Some states allow treble damages for security deposit violations"

**Injunction**
- **Definition**: A court order requiring someone to do or stop doing something
- **Example**: "An injunction could force the landlord to make repairs"

**Specific Performance**
- **Definition**: Requiring the defendant to do what they promised in a contract
- **Example**: "Seeking specific performance to force the landlord to fix the heating"

### Court Procedures

**Docket**
- **Definition**: The court's calendar or list of cases
- **Example**: "Your case will be assigned a docket number when filed"

**Voir Dire**
- **Definition**: The process of selecting a jury (not applicable in conciliation court)
- **Example**: "Small claims cases don't have juries, so there's no voir dire"

**Opening Statement**
- **Definition**: Your initial presentation to the judge explaining your case
- **Example**: "Keep your opening statement brief and focused on key facts"

**Direct Examination**
- **Definition**: Questioning your own witnesses
- **Example**: "During direct examination, ask open-ended questions"

**Cross-Examination**
- **Definition**: Questioning the other party's witnesses
- **Example**: "You can cross-examine the defendant's witnesses"

**Closing Argument**
- **Definition**: Your final summary of the case before the judge decides
- **Example**: "In closing arguments, summarize why the evidence supports your claims"

**Judgment**
- **Definition**: The court's final decision
- **Example**: "The judge will issue a judgment within 30 days"

**Appeal**
- **Definition**: Requesting a higher court to review the decision
- **Example**: "You can appeal a conciliation court decision to district court"

### Document Terms

**Pro Se / Pro Per**
- **Definition**: Representing yourself without a lawyer
- **Example**: "You're appearing pro se in conciliation court"

**Notarization**
- **Definition**: Official certification that a signature is authentic
- **Example**: "Some affidavits must be notarized by a notary public"

**Certified Copy**
- **Definition**: An official copy verified by the court clerk
- **Example**: "Request certified copies of court filings for your records"

**Exhibit**
- **Definition**: Evidence presented in court (documents, photos, etc.)
- **Example**: "Label each piece of evidence as Exhibit A, B, C, etc."

**Stipulation**
- **Definition**: An agreement between both parties about certain facts
- **Example**: "You can stipulate to basic facts to save time in court"

### Minnesota-Specific Terms

**Conciliation Court**
- **Definition**: Minnesota's small claims court for claims up to $15,000
- **Example**: "Conciliation court is faster and more informal than district court"

**Minn. Stat.**
- **Definition**: Abbreviation for Minnesota Statutes (state laws)
- **Example**: "Minn. Stat. ยง 504B.441 prohibits landlord retaliation"

**Housing Court**
- **Definition**: Specialized court division for landlord-tenant disputes
- **Example**: "Some counties have dedicated housing court calendars"

### Important Latin Terms

**Prima Facie**
- **Definition**: "At first sight" - evidence that appears sufficient unless contradicted
- **Example**: "Photos of violations establish a prima facie case"

**Res Judicata**
- **Definition**: A matter already decided by a court (can't re-litigate)
- **Example**: "If the court already ruled on this issue, it's res judicata"

**In Camera**
- **Definition**: In private, in the judge's chambers
- **Example**: "The judge may review sensitive documents in camera"

**Ex Parte**
- **Definition**: Done by or for one party only
- **Example**: "Ex parte communications with the judge are generally prohibited"

**Subpoena**
- **Definition**: A court order requiring someone to appear or produce documents
- **Example**: "You can request a subpoena for the landlord's repair records"

**Subpoena Duces Tecum**
- **Definition**: A subpoena requiring production of documents or evidence
- **Example**: "Use a subpoena duces tecum to get the landlord's bank records"

## Quick Tips for Using Legal Terms

1. **Don't overuse legal jargon** - Judges prefer clear, plain English
2. **Use terms correctly** - If you're not sure, use plain language instead
3. **Define terms when writing** - First use: "the plaintiff (Jeff Mills)"
4. **Stay professional** - Use proper terms, not slang or emotional language
5. **When in doubt, ask** - The court clerk can explain procedural terms

## Red Flag Terms (When You Might Need a Lawyer)

- **Criminal charges** - If the case involves potential crimes
- **Constitutional issues** - If your constitutional rights are at stake
- **Class action** - If representing multiple people
- **Complex legal theories** - If the case requires specialized legal knowledge
- **High stakes** - If you could lose significant money or property
- **Appeals** - Appellate procedure is complex
